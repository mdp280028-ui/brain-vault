# DXY Dollar Strength

The US Dollar Index (DXY) as a counter-indicator for risk assets. Cowen treats sustained DXY weakness as supportive for Bitcoin and SPX, and DXY strength as a headwind that compresses risk-asset valuations.

## Current Thesis

Cowen's DXY view has shifted from structurally benign to actively cautionary: while the political-cycle backdrop (Republican sweep, midterm year) historically favors dollar weakness, he now sees the dollar forming a large technical base and likely breaking out near-term — a directional reversal that would compound the rate-hike headwind already weighing on risk assets. The two views are in tension: the cyclical political regime argues for DXY softness, but the current technical and monetary setup argues for near-term strength. Cowen treats the likely breakout as an additional headwind for Bitcoin and SPX rather than the structural tailwind implied by the historical political-cycle pattern. The longer-term thesis that a Democratic Congress post-2026 midterms could flip the regime to dollar-supportive remains on watch.

## Key Evidence

- Republican sweep regimes have historically produced the largest DXY declines since 1980; the current political setup fits this pattern, implying structural dollar softness as the base case (9a-LA7tm8Mo)
- Midterm years are on average weak for the dollar — a trend obscured by the strong-dollar midterm years of 2018 and 2022 creating recency bias (9a-LA7tm8Mo)
- Democratic president + Republican Congress is the strongest dollar regime — flagged as a potential future headwind for risk assets if Congress flips post-2026 midterms (9a-LA7tm8Mo)
- Cowen identifies the dollar as forming a "massive base" on the chart and calls a breakout "likely" relatively soon, overriding the political-cycle softness expectation in the near term (zKPohkQdq_Q)
- A dollar breakout is cited alongside rising two-year yields and rate-hike pricing as a compound headwind for risk assets including Bitcoin, not a standalone observation (zKPohkQdq_Q)
- DXY is an active input to Cowen's macro framework, referenced alongside the ITC business cycle chart and presidential cycle (9a-LA7tm8Mo)
