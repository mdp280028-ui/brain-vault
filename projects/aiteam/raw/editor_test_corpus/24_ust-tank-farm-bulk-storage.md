A tank farm is a cluster of aboveground tanks storing bulk petroleum or fuel. Any facility exceeding 1,320 gallons of aggregate oil capacity must follow the Spill Prevention Control and Countermeasure regulation at 40 CFR 112 for inspection, containment, and compliance recordkeeping. A typical distribution site holds 50,000 to 10 million gallons across multiple tanks, while a coastal petroleum terminal can exceed 100 million gallons in one footprint.
Several thousand bulk fuel storage sites operate across the United States, ranging from private fleet yards of a few hundred thousand gallons to pipeline head terminals covering hundreds of acres. Most of these facilities mix aboveground storage tanks built to UL 142 or API 650 standards, connected through transfer piping, loading racks, and vapor recovery systems.
This guide covers how to distinguish a tank farm from a fuel depot or fuel terminal. It walks through the federal and state rules, secondary containment requirements, API 653 inspection, and upgrade or decommissioning work. Most readers run facilities between a 20,000 gallon bulk plant serving a regional fleet and a multi million gallon terminal feeding retail stations.
Built well, such a site runs quietly for decades unless one missed inspection turns the next release into a seven figure cleanup.
What is a tank farm and what gets stored there
A tank farm is distinguished from a single aboveground installation by scale, product mix, and operational function. The facility typically includes three or more storage tanks, onsite loading or transfer infrastructure, and dedicated spill response equipment. A single 5,000 gallon shop tank is not one. A 12 tank array at a regional distributor handling gasoline, diesel, and jet A absolutely is.
Stored products fall into four main categories. Refined petroleum fuels include gasoline, diesel, kerosene, jet A, and fuel oil. Crude oil sits at production or gathering points. Liquid petrochemicals cover ethanol and biodiesel, and non petroleum chemicals round out the list at industrial plants. The regulatory picture shifts by category, but SPCC at 40 CFR 112 covers every oil bearing liquid regardless of downstream use.
Typical tank sizes range from 10,000 gallon horizontal shop vessels to 400,000 barrel (roughly 17 million gallon) floating roof tanks at major terminals. Private fleet yards operating their own bulk petroleum storage often run 10,000 to 50,000 gallon vertical aboveground storage tanks. Cardlock distributors typically run 20,000 to 150,000 gallon tanks. Pipeline head stations and refinery adjacent terminals run the largest vessels in the inventory.
Product throughput matters as much as nameplate capacity. A 200,000 gallon tank cycling weekly moves more product per year than a 1 million gallon tank turning twice annually. Inspection frequency, loading rack traffic, and release risk all scale with throughput rather than with static capacity. Operators planning a new bulk fuel storage layout should model turnover before choosing tank sizes.
How tank terminology actually splits across the industry
The terms tank farm, fuel depot, and fuel terminal overlap heavily in trade usage but carry different technical meanings. A fuel depot typically refers to a smaller distribution point that receives product by truck and redistributes to retail stations or fleet customers within a local radius. Depot capacity usually falls between 200,000 gallons and 5 million gallons.
A fuel terminal receives product by pipeline, barge, rail, or ocean tanker and distributes it via truck rack, rail car, or outbound pipeline. Terminal capacity typically starts at 10 million gallons and can exceed 300 million gallons at the largest coastal sites. The American Petroleum Institute and the U.S. Energy Information Administration track terminal inventory at the state and product level through the Petroleum Supply Monthly.
Tank farm is the broader label for any large grouping of storage tanks. The term applies whether the site functions as a depot, a refinery day tank array, or a pipeline pump station. The phrase describes the physical arrangement rather than the business function. An industrial chemical plant with 30 tanks holding process intermediates runs one even if it never sells a gallon to an outside customer.
Regulators tend to prefer fuel storage facility or bulk storage site in rule text. EPA 40 CFR 112 uses aggregate oil capacity thresholds instead of depot or terminal categories. State aboveground storage tank programs vary: Texas regulates larger sites through TCEQ, while Louisiana oversees bulk storage via LDEQ permit programs. Operators should follow rule text rather than vernacular when filing.
Federal and state regulatory framework for bulk storage sites
The primary federal rule for aboveground petroleum storage is the Spill Prevention Control and Countermeasure rule at 40 CFR 112. EPA first promulgated it in 1973 under Clean Water Act 1972 authority. SPCC applies to any non transportation fuel storage facility with aggregate aboveground oil capacity exceeding 1,320 gallons. The rule mandates a written SPCC plan, engineered secondary containment, inspection and recordkeeping, and personnel training.
Facilities with aggregate aboveground capacity over 10,000 gallons require an SPCC plan certified by a licensed professional engineer. Smaller tier facilities between 1,320 and 10,000 gallons may self certify under the Tier I or Tier II templates EPA publishes at its SPCC guidance page. The 10,000 gallon PE threshold is one of the most expensive triggers in the federal framework because engineer stamped plans typically run $5,000 to $25,000.
Underground components at mixed sites fall under 40 CFR 280, the UST rule. Aboveground construction must comply with NFPA 30 Flammable and Combustible Liquids Code for fire safety, OSHA 1910.106 for workplace flammable liquid handling, and API 650 for welded steel tank construction. API 653 governs in service inspection, repair, alteration, and reconstruction of existing aboveground tanks. These are the four pillars of the aboveground framework.
State aboveground programs vary widely. Some states adopt SPCC by reference with minor additions, while others operate parallel registration, permit, and fee programs. Facilities in Texas or Louisiana often carry federal SPCC, state AST registration, and local fire marshal clearance simultaneously. Facility response plans under 40 CFR 112.20 kick in separately at 1 million gallons aggregate capacity with navigable water proximity.
Secondary containment and spill prevention at scale
Secondary containment for large aboveground tanks must hold at least 100 percent of the largest single tank within the containment area. It must also provide freeboard for a 25 year, 24 hour rainfall event, per 40 CFR 112.7(c). At a four tank bulk fuel storage array with a 500,000 gallon tank as the largest, the engineered capacity must exceed 500,000 gallons plus the calculated rain volume. That single requirement drives most containment design choices at working sites.
Reinforced concrete dikes remain the default for permanent bulk plants because they resist heavy equipment traffic, last 40 plus years, and accept engineered drain valves and oil water separators. Concrete dike construction runs $40 to $90 per square foot of wall face, with total system cost driven mostly by wall height and soil preparation rather than wall length. Earthen berms with high density polyethylene liners cost less at $4 to $12 per square foot but require vegetation management and periodic liner replacement.
Double walled aboveground storage tanks meeting UL 2085 protected tank standards can replace external secondary containment for smaller installations, typically under 50,000 gallons per tank. The integral interstitial space serves as the secondary barrier and simplifies site layout. Protected tanks cost 30 to 60 percent more than equivalent single walled UL 142 tanks but avoid the concrete work entirely. This tradeoff is a common decision point in new fuel depot construction.
