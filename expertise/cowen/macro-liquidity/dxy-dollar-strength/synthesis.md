# DXY Dollar Strength

The US Dollar Index (DXY) as a counter-indicator for risk assets. Cowen treats sustained DXY weakness as supportive for Bitcoin and SPX, and DXY strength as a headwind that compresses risk-asset valuations.

## Current Thesis

Cowen's near-term DXY view has firmed into a specific directional call: the dollar bottomed in early 2026 (mirroring its Trump-first-term trough in early 2018) and is now grinding higher toward the 105–106 area, a pattern Cowen says he has been calling all year. This near-term dollar strength is treated as a sustained macro headwind for crypto into late 2026, compounding the macro shifts — rising inflation concerns, deteriorating unemployment — that have suppressed risk assets throughout the Biden and Trump-second-term cycles alike. The political-cycle backdrop (Republican sweep, midterm year) still implies structural dollar softness as a longer-term base case, but that thesis is subordinated to the cyclical analog Cowen is now tracking: DXY topped at inauguration, troughed roughly a year later, and is now in a multi-month recovery phase. The potential for a Democratic Congress post-2026 midterms to flip the regime to dollar-supportive remains a watch item.

## Key Evidence

- DXY followed the same cycle template across Trump's first and second terms: topped January 2017 / January 2025, bottomed January–February 2018 / February 2026, then began grinding higher — Cowen has been calling for this dollar rise all year (OYcGIifaO4Q)
- Cowen targets the 105–106 area as the next DXY destination and explicitly frames continued dollar strength as a macro headwind for crypto in the second half of 2026 (OYcGIifaO4Q)
- Cowen identified the dollar forming a "massive base" on the chart and called a breakout "likely" relatively soon, overriding the political-cycle softness expectation in the near term (zKPohkQdq_Q)
- A dollar breakout was cited alongside rising two-year yields and rate-hike pricing as a compound headwind for risk assets including Bitcoin, not a standalone observation (zKPohkQdq_Q)
- Republican sweep regimes have historically produced the largest DXY declines since 1980; midterm years are on average weak for the dollar — structural softness remains the longer-term base case even as near-term direction is higher (9a-LA7tm8Mo)
- Democratic president + Republican Congress is the strongest dollar regime — flagged as a potential future headwind for risk assets if Congress flips post-2026 midterms (9a-LA7tm8Mo)
