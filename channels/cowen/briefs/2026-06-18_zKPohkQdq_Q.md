## Key points (the distilled video)
1. Warsh's first FOMC statement was dramatically shorter than Powell-era releases — roughly half a page vs. nearly a page and a half — signaling a deliberate move away from forward guidance toward plain fact-stating.
2. Warsh did not submit his own dot plot entry, but nine committee members indicated they expect a rate hike before year-end. The CME FedWatch tool shows the market pricing only a 15.5% chance that rates are NOT hiked by December — meaning an ~84.5% implied probability of at least one hike.
3. Cowen frames inflation as "a choice" — it has been above the 2% target continuously since 2021, never actually touching 2% even at its lowest, and the political will to allow asset prices to fall (the most direct cure) is absent.
4. The two-year yield is heading back up, which historically leads or forces Fed rate hikes. Cowen treats a sustained elevated two-year as a near-mechanical forcing function on the Fed funds rate, citing the 1960s and late-1990s as precedents.
5. Rate hike expectations — whether a hike materializes or not — are themselves a headwind for Bitcoin and the broader altcoin market, which Cowen says has underperformed everything this year. Loose monetary policy is a precondition for crypto outperformance; tighter conditions suppress it.
6. Cowen notes the dollar appears to be forming a large base and looks likely to break out relatively soon, which he identifies as an additional headwind for risk assets broadly.
7. The 10-year yield is likely heading back to its October 2023 highs, per Cowen's long-standing view. The 30-year yield has already reached that level — the 10-year is expected to follow.
8. Cowen raises the historical pattern of the S&P 500 testing new Fed chairs: after a new chair assumes the role, the market often "throws them a curveball." He speculates the back-half midterm-year correction could be that test for Warsh — and would reveal whether he hikes into higher inflation or runs things hot.
9. If the Fed opts to run things hot (not hike or even cut), bond vigilantes would push the long end of the yield curve higher regardless, reinforcing his thesis that long yields are headed up either way.
10. On energy stocks: XLE historically tops after the stock market does (citing May 2008 vs. October 2007 peak, and May 2001 vs. March 2000 peak). Energy has pulled back with oil prices recently, but Cowen expects it to reassert higher unless a durable equity recession is underway.

## Predictions made
- The 10-year yield is likely heading back up to its October 2023 highs (the 30-year has already done so)
- The dollar is likely breaking out of a large base formation relatively soon, acting as a headwind for risk assets
- The S&P 500 may test Warsh with a correction in the back half of the midterm year (H2 2026)
