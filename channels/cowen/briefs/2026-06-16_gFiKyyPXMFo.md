## Key points (the distilled video)
1. SpaceX IPO'd in June 2026 and immediately surged ~50% from its opening price. Cowen notes he tweeted at launch to expect a post-IPO price surge lasting anywhere from one day to a few weeks.
2. The classic pattern for successful IPOs is: surge at launch → pullback → more durable long-term move. Cowen illustrates this with Tesla (IPO June 2010, +60% then -51% within one month), Microsoft (surged, dropped ~30%, never revisited that low), Apple (multiple large post-IPO drops of 70–80%), and Nvidia (surged, corrected into H2, then another massive move up).
3. The Tesla–SpaceX parallel is the video's central analogy: both companies led by Elon Musk, both IPO'd in June of a midterm year (Tesla June 2010, SpaceX June 2026 — exactly 16 years apart). Tesla was up ~60% from its IPO open; SpaceX is currently up ~50%.
4. After Tesla's 60% post-IPO surge it dropped 51% — all within approximately one month — and never traded below that low again. Cowen sees this as the template for what SpaceX could do.
5. The counter-example shown is Rivian: it also surged ~60–70% from its IPO open, but then dropped 95% and never recovered. Cowen uses this to reinforce that IPO surges alone don't guarantee success — leadership and product quality matter.
6. The macro catalyst for a SpaceX pullback would be the standard midterm-year equity correction in H2. Cowen cites 2022, 2018, and 2014 as examples where stocks corrected in both H1 and H2 of midterm years, arguing a second-half correction is the norm.
7. Cowen's long-term thesis is simply a bet on Elon Musk as an operator: having proven himself at Tesla, he sees no reason to assume SpaceX will be the exception. "A lot of people have lost a lot of money betting against Elon Musk."
8. He explicitly frames any H2 drawdown as a buying opportunity for investors with a multi-year horizon, not a reason to avoid the stock. He cautions that anyone who cannot tolerate 30–50% drawdowns in individual stocks should not be investing in them.

## Predictions made
